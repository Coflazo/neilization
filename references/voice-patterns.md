# Voice Patterns

This reference distills the usable high-level patterns from the local corpus in `/Users/pc/Desktop/Neil degrasse Tyson`: books, essays, testimony, interviews, official astronomy papers, reports, and transcripts. It must not be used to recreate a living author's style. Use it to guide a lawful public-science register.

## Corpus Map

- **Public science books and essays**: strongest source for voice patterns. These show scale translation, cosmic perspective, analogies, dry asides, evidence sorting, and movement from everyday examples to large-scale consequences.
- **Starry Messenger and civic essays**: strongest source for public-policy and culture transformations. These frequently use outsider vantage points, category tests, continua, and the distinction between objective claims and personal or political claims.
- **Senate testimony**: useful for public argument. It compresses history, national ambition, budget comparison, and institutional stakes into direct persuasive prose.
- **Interviews and transcripts**: useful for spoken clarity. They show the principle of going to the audience's existing interests before attaching science.
- **Astronomy research papers**: useful for precision, not personality. They use conventional abstracts, methods, data limits, confidence language, citations, and measured conclusions.
- **Commission reports**: institutional register. Use for policy clarity and structure, not voice.

## Macrostructure

### 1. Start With A Handle

Open with something the reader can enter immediately: a date, public event, familiar object, common phrase, observed habit, pop-culture reference, or simple question already implicit in the topic.

Constructed example:

```text
Before you can ask whether artificial intelligence is intelligent, you have to ask what job the word "intelligent" is doing.
```

Use this when the source opens with a generic abstraction. Replace "Technology has transformed modern society" with a specific scene, mechanism, or claim.

### 2. Widen The Lens In Stages

The movement usually goes:

1. Familiar object or social habit.
2. Scientific mechanism or measurement.
3. Larger scale: planet, species, history, galaxy, or universe.
4. Return to the human consequence.

Do not skip from a local fact straight to cosmic grandeur. The bridge is the point.

### 3. Make Scale Behave

Numbers need translation. Use comparisons that preserve the order of magnitude and help the reader feel the claim.

Good tactics:

- Convert budget numbers into pennies per dollar, days of spending, or per-person costs.
- Convert cosmic distances into travel time, light travel time, or nested maps.
- Convert microscopic quantities into ratios against familiar objects.
- Convert time into a sequence of named intervals.

Bad tactics:

- "Astronomically large" without a comparison.
- A string of digits with no consequence.
- Comparisons that sound vivid but distort scale.

### 4. Explain Invisible Things By Their Effects

For invisible or abstract entities, name the observable consequence.

Use:

- Dark matter: what it does to galaxy rotation and gravitational lensing.
- Black holes: what they do to stars, gas, light, and clocks.
- Algorithms: what they do to examples, error, prediction, and incentives.
- Institutions: what they do to budgets, behavior, incentives, and trust.

The pattern is not "believe in X." It is "visible Y behaves as though X is present."

### 5. Sort Claims By Evidence Type

When the source blurs truth, belief, opinion, identity, politics, and evidence, separate them.

Categories to use:

- Measured.
- Inferred.
- Modeled.
- Personally believed.
- Politically asserted.
- Pre-consensus.
- Unknown because data are missing.
- Unknown because the mechanism is missing.

This is central to the civic mode. It lets the prose be firm without becoming preachy.

### 6. Use The Outsider Test

For civic and cultural topics, ask what a neutral observer would count, see, misread, or report. The observer can be an alien, a future historian, a child, a scientist from another field, or a person seeing the system from orbit.

Use the device only when it reveals a contradiction:

- Humans claim a category is natural, but measurements show a continuum.
- A society claims liberty while restricting another person's expression.
- People fear a tiny risk while accepting a larger familiar risk.
- Institutions praise science while starving the science pipeline.

### 7. Replace Scolding With Arithmetic

When correcting a misconception, let the measurement expose it.

Instead of:

```text
This belief is ignorant and anti-science.
```

Use:

```text
The claim fails the first measurement. If it were true, X would change by Y, and it does not.
```

Precision cools the temperature of an argument.

### 8. Let Humor Ride On Explanation

Dry humor works when it arrives after the reader understands the mechanism. It should be short, incidental, and earned.

Constructed example:

```text
The model has not developed wisdom. It has learned which answer usually follows which prompt, a skill that also describes many committee meetings.
```

Do not write joke-first paragraphs. Do not stack wry asides. Do not punch down.

### 9. Use Time As Structure

When the source explains a process, make time visible:

- "First..."
- "By the time..."
- "After..."
- "At that point..."
- "A century later..."

For long process explanations, a standalone time marker can orient the reader. Do this sparingly.

### 10. Prefer Four Honest Items To Three Decorative Ones

The corpus often uses enumeration, but the number should come from the material. If there are two causes, use two. If there are four stages, use four. Avoid the automatic three-part cadence that makes prose sound packaged.

### 11. Close On Consequence, Not Slogan

Good closers land on:

- A concrete image.
- A measured consequence.
- A reversal of perspective.
- A small domestic object that carries the large claim.
- A question the reader now understands better.

Bad closers:

- "Only time will tell."
- "This is more important than ever."
- "The future is bright."
- "It serves as a reminder."

## Sentence-Level Habits

- Use direct declarative sentences after long explanations.
- Define terms once, inline, without "in other words" announcements.
- Repeat precise technical terms when needed; do not synonym-cycle.
- Use "we" for humanity, shared civilization, or science as a collective effort.
- Use "you" when the reader is being asked to picture or test something.
- Use "I" only when the source author actually has a first-person stance.
- Use contractions in spoken or essay register; avoid them in formal reports.
- Use questions when they create inquiry, not as clickbait setup.

## Register Notes

### Public Essay

More room for analogy, direct address, wry aside, and cosmic scale. Paragraphs may be medium-long if the reasoning is moving.

### Civic Essay

The prose can be sharper. It should classify claims, expose contradictions, and move from tribal categories to species-level or evidence-level categories.

### Testimony

Shorter paragraphs. More budget comparisons, historical parallels, and institutional consequences. The goal is persuasion before a public body, not wonder for its own sake.

### Research Paper

No personality-forward moves. Preserve method, sample, uncertainty, citation, and limitation. Improve clarity through structure and specificity.

### Interview

Keep the answer direct. Use examples the audience already understands, then attach the science to that scaffold.

## Rewrite Recipe

1. Name the real subject of the piece.
2. Name the reader's likely misconception or missing bridge.
3. Choose one scale shift.
4. Choose one concrete analogy.
5. Decide what can be cut.
6. Decide what must be added for accuracy.
7. Draft in paragraphs, not line-by-line paraphrase.
8. Run the structural and vocabulary references.
